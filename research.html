Research

This page is intentionally paragraph based. It explains what physics the laboratory investigates in a way that is understandable to a general academic audience without becoming too technical.

Shape coexistence in atomic nuclei

Some nuclei can show different internal shapes at nearly the same energy. The laboratory studies how and why these competing structures appear, and what they reveal about the changing organisation of protons and neutrons inside the nucleus.

Nuclear structure away from stability

Nuclei with unusual neutron-to-proton balance often behave differently from stable nuclei found in everyday matter. By examining such systems, the lab explores how shell structure, collectivity, and excitation patterns evolve under extreme conditions.

Gamma-ray spectroscopy

When excited nuclei release energy, they emit gamma rays that carry information about their internal states. The lab uses detector-based measurements to reconstruct level schemes and understand the sequence of transitions between nuclear energy levels.

Experimental signatures of collective motion

Atomic nuclei can rotate, vibrate, or display other collective behaviours involving many nucleons at once. The research group investigates these patterns to connect measured observables with the larger-scale motion of the nucleus as a quantum many-body system.

Experiment and model comparison

Measurements become more meaningful when they are interpreted alongside established theoretical ideas and nuclear models. The laboratory therefore places experimental findings in a broader framework to better understand structure trends across isotopes and regions of the nuclear chart.
